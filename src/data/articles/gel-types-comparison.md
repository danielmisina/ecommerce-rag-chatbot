# Gels, Chews, and Drink Mixes: Which Should You Use?
Triathlon nutrition comes in three main formats: gels, chews, and drink mixes. Each has a place depending on the discipline, race duration, and athlete preference.
## Energy Gels
Gels are the most common race fuel: compact, fast to consume, and easy to carry in a race belt. They deliver 20-30g of carbohydrate per sachet.
Isotonic gels such as SiS Beta Fuel and Maurten Gel 100 are pre-mixed to match your body's fluid concentration. No need for extra water. Best for athletes prone to GI issues.
Hypertonic gels such as GU Energy Gel and High5 Energy Gel are more concentrated. Always chase with 150-200 ml of water to dilute. Taking hypertonic gels without water is a leading cause of gut problems in racing.
Real food gels such as Spring Energy Canaberry use whole ingredients like oats, fruit, and rice. Slower digestion but gentler on sensitive stomachs. Popular in ultra-endurance and adventure races.
## Energy Chews
Chews like Clif Shot Bloks deliver carbs in a solid, chewable format. Many athletes find them more satisfying than gels on long bike legs. They are slightly slower to absorb than liquid gels and require more saliva to process, so they are not ideal during very high-intensity efforts.
## Drink Mixes
Products like Maurten Drink Mix 320 and Tailwind Endurance Fuel combine carbohydrates and electrolytes in a single drink. The advantage is simplicity: one bottle covers both fuelling and hydration.
Maurten uses hydrogel technology which may reduce GI distress at higher carbohydrate intakes. Tailwind is formulated to be the only fuel needed, eliminating gels entirely for athletes who prefer a simpler strategy.
## Matching Format to Discipline
For the bike leg, use a combination of drink mix and gels every 45 minutes. For the run, gels at aid stations work best. Avoid solid chews when running above Zone 3 intensity. During the swim no nutrition is needed.
## Practical Tips
- Carry gels in a Compressport race belt with gel loops for fast access
- Alternate flavours to avoid nausea over long events
- Caffeine gels are best saved for the second half of the run when fatigue peaks
- Always open gel packets in training to build muscle memory for race day
- For a 70.3 bike leg of around 3 hours at 60g carbs per hour you need roughly 7-8 gels of 25g carbs each
