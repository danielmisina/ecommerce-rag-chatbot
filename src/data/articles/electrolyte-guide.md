# Electrolytes in Triathlon: Sodium, Magnesium, and Sweat Rate
Electrolyte management is the silent performance lever in long-course triathlon. Dehydration gets the headlines, but sodium imbalance causes more race-day disasters than most athletes realise.
## Why Sodium Matters
Sodium is the primary electrolyte in sweat. It regulates fluid balance between cells and plasma. In a long-course triathlon on a hot day, athletes can lose 1000-2500 mg of sodium per hour through sweat, far more than plain water or low-sodium sports drinks can replace.
Hyponatremia - dangerously low blood sodium - is a documented cause of hospitalisation in Ironman racing. It occurs when athletes drink large amounts of plain water without replacing sodium, diluting plasma concentration. Symptoms include nausea, headache, confusion, and in severe cases seizures.
Signs you are under-replacing sodium include swelling in hands and feet late in a race, persistent nausea not explained by over-fuelling, mental fogginess in the final run kilometres, and calf or hamstring cramps that do not respond to stretching.
## Sweat Rate and Sodium Concentration
Both your sweat rate and your sweat sodium concentration are largely genetic. Salty sweaters who see white residue on their skin after exercise lose significantly more sodium than average and need aggressive replacement.
A simple DIY test: weigh yourself before and after a 60-minute session with no fluid intake. Every 1 kg lost equals approximately 1 litre of sweat.

## Magnesium
Magnesium is involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions including muscle contraction and energy metabolism. Deficiency is common in endurance athletes due to sweat losses and high training loads. Symptoms include muscle cramps, poor sleep, and elevated resting heart rate.
Good sources include dark leafy greens, nuts, dark chocolate, and magnesium supplements taken at night.
## Race-Day Electrolyte Strategy
For sprint and Olympic distance under 3 hours, a standard isotonic drink or High5 ZERO tab in your bottle is sufficient. Sodium depletion is not a major risk in shorter events.
For 70.3 races lasting 3-6 hours, aim for 500-800 mg sodium per hour from drink mix, gel content, and electrolyte tabs combined.
For Ironman lasting 8-17 hours, target 800-1500 mg sodium per hour depending on conditions and sweat rate. In heat above 28 degrees Celsius, increase toward the higher end. ## Hot vs Cold Racing
In cold conditions below 15 degrees Celsius, sweating is reduced and plain water with a moderate electrolyte drink is sufficient. The bigger cold-weather risk is under-drinking due to absent thirst signals - drink on a schedule regardless of whether you feel thirsty.
In hot conditions the electrolyte strategy described above applies. Acclimatise by training in heat 2-3 weeks before a hot-weather race to expand plasma volume and improve sweat efficiency.
