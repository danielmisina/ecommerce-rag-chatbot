# Pre-Race Nutrition: What to Eat Before a Triathlon
What you eat in the 48 hours before race day has a significant impact on your performance. Proper carbohydrate loading, hydration, and a calm race-morning routine set you up for a strong start.
## Carbohydrate Loading
For events over 90 minutes, carbohydrate loading - deliberately increasing glycogen stores - is evidence-based and worthwhile.
48 hours before the race, increase carbohydrate intake to 8-10g per kg of body weight per day. For a 70 kg athlete that means 560-700g carbs per day. Use sources like white rice, pasta, bread, potatoes, and bananas. Reduce training to a short shake-out session with no long or hard workouts.
24 hours before the race, continue high-carb intake but reduce fibre. Avoid whole grains, legumes, and cruciferous vegetables. Stay well hydrated with pale yellow urine as the target. Limit alcohol entirely as it impairs glycogen storage and disrupts sleep.
## The Night Before
A moderate carbohydrate meal, low in fat and fibre, 3-4 hours before you plan to sleep. Good examples are white pasta with tomato sauce and chicken, rice with grilled fish and steamed carrots, or a Veloforte recovery bar as a pre-bed snack if hungry.
Avoid anything unfamiliar, overly rich, or spicy. Your digestive system does not need a challenge the night before your A-race.
## Race Morning
Wake up 2.5-3 hours before your race start. Eat within 30 minutes of waking: 1-2g carbs per kg of body weight, which is 70-140g for a 70 kg athlete. Use low fat, low fibre, low protein, fast-digesting foods. A good example is 2 slices of white toast with honey, a banana, and a sports drink.
Stay hydrated with water and electrolytes. A High5 ZERO tab or Precision Hydration sachet in 500 ml water 60-90 minutes before the start primes your sodium levels without overloading your gut.
In the final 15-20 minutes before the swim start, take a caffeinated gel for a mental and physical primer. Time it 30-45 minutes before you expect peak intensity on the bike.
## Sprint vs Long-Course Differences
For sprint distance under 2 hours, formal carbohydrate loading is not necessary. A normal, slightly higher-carb dinner the night before and a standard race-morning meal is sufficient. Focus on sleep quality and race-morning calm rather than precise gram counts.
For 70.3 and Ironman, the loading protocol matters. Every extra gram of glycogen stored means more available fuel for the run, where carbohydrate depletion - hitting the wall - is most likely to occur.
