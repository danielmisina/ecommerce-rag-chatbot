# How to Fuel an Ironman: Bike and Run Nutrition
Completing an Ironman triathlon demands a precise fuelling strategy. Most age-group athletes are on course for 10-17 hours, meaning nutrition is not optional; it is the fourth discipline.
## Carbohydrate Targets
Your body can absorb roughly 60g of carbohydrate per hour using glucose alone. With a trained gut using dual-source fuels (glucose + fructose in a 2:1 ratio), athletes can push 80-90g per hour. Start conservatively at 60g/hr and build up through training.
On the bike leg (typically 5-7 hours for age-groupers):
- Aim for 60-80g carbs per hour from gel, chews, or drink mix
- Take your first gel at 30 minutes, then every 30-45 minutes thereafter
- Combine solid and liquid sources to avoid flavour fatigue on long rides
On the run leg (typically 3-6 hours):
- Drop to 40-60g carbs per hour - your stomach is more stressed running
- Use caffeinated gels in the second half of the run for a mental and physical boost
- Aid station cola in the final 10 km is a race-proven strategy: fast sugars plus caffeine
## Hydration
Drink to thirst rather than to a fixed schedule. In hot conditions aim for 500-750 ml per hour on the bike. Use electrolyte tabs or high-sodium drink mix to replace sweat losses. Precision Hydration PH 1500 is a solid choice for heavy sweaters.
## Gut Training
Your gut is trainable. Practice race-intensity fuelling on every long brick session. Your stomach adapts to processing carbohydrates and fluids at high effort over 8-12 weeks of consistent training nutrition.
## Transition Nutrition
T1 (after swim): eat a gel immediately when you mount the bike - your body is in deficit after the swim.
T2 (after bike): take a gel or cola as you start the run to bridge the gap while your stomach adjusts.
## How Many Gels Do I Need?
For a 6-hour Ironman bike leg at 60g carbs per hour you need 360g of carbohydrate total. With a 25g carb gel like Maurten Gel 100 that equals 15 gels. At 80g carbs per hour (advanced gut training) you would need 20 gels for the same duration. Always carry 2-3 extra gels as a buffer for slower-than-planned race days.
For the run leg at 50g carbs per hour over 5 hours you need 250g carbohydrate, roughly 10 gels. Use SiS Beta Fuel or GU Energy Gel at aid stations every 30-40 minutes.
## Common Mistakes
- Starting the run in a calorie deficit because you under-fuelled the bike
- Taking too many gels at once to catch up - this causes GI distress
- Ignoring sodium and relying only on water, leading to hyponatremia in hot races
- Trying new products on race day - always test in training first
