# How to Prevent GI Distress in Triathlon Racing
Gastrointestinal problems such as nausea, cramping, vomiting, and diarrhoea are among the most common causes of a DNF or a ruined race time. The good news is that most GI issues are preventable.
## Why GI Distress Happens
During high-intensity exercise, blood is diverted away from the gut to working muscles. The digestive system slows down dramatically. Introducing large amounts of food, hypertonic solutions, or unfamiliar products overwhelms an already-compromised system.
Key triggers include hypertonic gels taken without water, eating or drinking too much at once, novel foods on race day that have not been tested in training, excessive fibre or fat close to race time, and pre-race anxiety elevating cortisol levels.
## The 3 Rules of Race Nutrition
Rule one: nothing new on race day. Every product must be tested in training at race pace. Your first time with Maurten Hydrogel should not be at kilometre 80 of your Ironman bike.
Rule two: small, frequent doses. Taking 3 gels at once to catch up on missed fuelling is worse than the deficit itself. Aim for 20-30g carbs every 20-30 minutes rather than large boluses.
Rule three: chase concentrated products with water. Hypertonic gels need dilution. Use the aid station water 2-3 minutes after a gel if you did not drink alongside it.
## Gut Training
Your gut is an organ that adapts. Over an 8-12 week build period, practise ingesting carbohydrates at race intensity on every session over 90 minutes. Start at 40g per hour and progressively increase. Athletes who gut-train can comfortably absorb 80-90g per hour that would otherwise cause distress.
## Pre-Race Day Strategies
Eat your last large meal 12-14 hours before the race start. On race morning eat a simple, low-fibre meal 2-3 hours before: white rice, banana, or white bread with jam. Avoid high-fat, high-fibre, and high-dairy foods for 24 hours before the race. Stay hydrated the evening before using pale yellow urine as the target.
## On-Course Management
If nausea develops during the bike leg, back off slightly to let the gut recover. Sip plain water rather than sugary drink mix. Switch from gels to flat cola or banana if available at aid stations since these are easier to process. Do not stop fuelling entirely as deepening the energy deficit makes the run leg worse.
Strong electrolyte products like Precision Hydration PH 1500 can reduce nausea in hot conditions by maintaining plasma sodium levels.
